# DKA Potassium Management

## Why Potassium Is Critical in DKA

- Total body potassium is **always depleted** in DKA (deficit: 3–5 mEq/kg)
- Serum K⁺ may appear normal or high on admission due to acidosis-driven extracellular shift
- Insulin therapy drives K⁺ back into cells → rapid drop → life-threatening hypokalemia

## Replacement Protocol

| Serum K⁺ | Action |
|----------|--------|
| < 3.3 mEq/L | **Hold insulin.** Replace K⁺ aggressively (20–40 mEq/hr IV) until K⁺ > 3.3 |
| 3.3–5.3 mEq/L | Add 20–30 mEq K⁺ per liter of IV fluid. Start/continue insulin |
| > 5.3 mEq/L | Do NOT give K⁺. Recheck every 2 hours. Start insulin |

## Monitoring Schedule

| Phase | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|
| Initial (first 4–6 hours) | Every 1–2 hours |
| Stabilized | Every 2–4 hours |
| Post-resolution | Every 4–6 hours until stable |

## Clinical Alerts

- **ECG monitoring** required when K⁺ < 3.0 or > 6.0 mEq/L
- **Never start insulin** when K⁺ < 3.3 — risk of fatal arrhythmia
- **Renal function** affects replacement — reduce rate if oliguric/anuric
- **Oral replacement** preferred once patient tolerates oral intake
